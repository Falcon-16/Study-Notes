Second derivatives represent how the ‘change’ occurs. If a function maps the displacement, the first derivative represents the speed, and the second derivative represents the acceleration.
To calculate the second derivative, just calculate the derivative of the derivative; do the derivative maths twice.
The second derivative will tell you the concavity; the way that the graph ‘points’ or ‘curves’.

Consider the following graph:

The second derivative describes the following:

What the sign of the second derivative shows is which way is being pushed. Imagine a rolling car. A negative second derivative means the car is being pushed/accelerated backwards. It could be rolling forwards and being slowed down, and then backing up. A positive second derivative means the car is being pushed/accelerated forwards. It could be rolling backwards, slowing down, and then rolling forwards.

This brings us to the second derivative test, which can quickly identify if a point of inflection is a local maximum, or a local minimum.
